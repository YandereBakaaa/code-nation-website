Taking risks and how it affects projects.

A risk doesn't mean a make or break situation every time but it can still make an impact on a project if something fails to happen, such as missed deadlines can deal a big blow to a project depending on how important that deadline was. There are many risks that exist during an entire project and it's important to be aware of them as you move along, an example of some of these risks:

  • Performance risk, the risk that the project will fail to produce results consistent with project specifications.
  • Schedule risk, the risk that activities will take longer than expected. Slippages in schedule typically increase costs and, also, delay the receipt of project benefits, with a possible loss of competitive advantage.
  • Cost risk, typically escalation of project costs due to poor cost estimating accuracy and scope creep.

Selecting the right tool for the job is important.

There are a lot of factors to take in when deciding which software you need to use for your project, an important aspect is the management tools you have a vast selection of, all offering their own pros and cons.

  • Collaboration

    • Collaboration is a very important point to consider depending on your project, having the ability of multiple people working together seamlessly is a fantastic concept. However sometimes you don't need that kind of feature if you're doing a solo project in which case a different tool with other focuses may be a better shot.

  • Ease of use

    • An easy to use program is always better for most people and that's no exception when it comes to project management, a big complicated program can be frustrating to figure out or just downright demotivating so it's important to make sure what you're using is clear and concise with its functions.

  • Integration

    • If you already have an existing product you need to think about how a new software will impact the day to day tasks you currently do, it's important to understand a new product inside and out before making the switch as most of the time you might not realise what will be affected until it's too late.

    These examples are just to name a few but the conditions for required software can vary so much it's impossible to give a solution that fits all, it's important to take in all factors before finally committing to a product.

What is GDPR and how will it affect a project?

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) is an EU legislation that regulates personal data that has been stored and how it can be used by businesses. One of the primary objectives of GDPR is adjusting the legal regulations concerning the processing and protection of personal data to better fit the challenges of technological development and globalisation which have facilitated the unprecedented availability and pervasive use of personal data. Just to be clear, by personal data I mean any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person—including identifiers such as a name, an identification number, location data, an online identifier, or, for example, factors specific to the physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural person.[1]

Minimising the effect of GDPR on a project.

Since a service provider cannot avoid being subject to GDPR by changing its location, the only way to minimise the impact of these new regulations is by minimising the scope of the processing—for example by collecting less personal data, avoiding the processing of sensitive data categories (including health records, data on racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, etc.), and limiting the scope of purposes of the processing and transfers of personal data to other entities.
